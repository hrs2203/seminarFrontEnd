Overview

Sudden and persistent stresses in the environment threaten the consistent availability and quality of shared natural resources (land, water, air, food) in rural communities over a long period of time, and need assistance because many of these disturbances often exacerbate problems of natural disasters (droughts, floods), crop losses, food insecurity, water availability, and rural poverty. Robust solutions to these problems warrant collaboration and coordination among all stakeholders (from landowner to policymaker) so that the nexus is managed effectively and efficiently. By promoting development and use of innovative participatory decision support and modeling technologies focused on identifying food-energy-water nexus in communities, stakeholders will be empowered to collaborate and self-identify innovative, on-the-ground decisions in all three sectors that work and are resilient to short- and long-term impacts of climate change (e.g., droughts and floods). In this context, using a nexus perspective for resources management in India will provide opportunities for identifying synergies in stakeholders of these governmental programs, as well as identify opportunities for industry collaboration in development of novel technologies that enable combined food-energy-water security in Indian communities. In summary, the need for an action-oriented research that involves stakeholders, scientists, policymakers and non-governmental organizations (NGOs), and also advances the use and development of cyberinfrastructure to support participatory approaches is clear. It is necessary that our rural communities in India have access and training to use information and communication technologies as a mode of remote communication between researchers, extension agents and farmers, as well as for coordinating farm-level to watershed-scale decision support to manage uncertainties posed by the future climate.

Scope

‘From Knowledge to Action: A Vision for Resilient Rural India’ will be a focusing on the science and practice of managing the interdependencies (nexus) among food, energy and water (FEW) in rural watersheds, and on utilizing the understanding of this nexus for building resilient and sustainable communities constrained by an uncertain future climate. Applications of stakeholder engagement approaches, watershed hydrological models, informatics techniques and methods, and decision support systems to support interdependent food, energy, and water resources in rural watersheds of India will be addressed in this workshop. The various tools utilized in modeling the nexus approach and the pathways to resiliency (in the context of droughts, floods, crop yield loss, economic loss) that could be adopted at a watershed-scale will be discussed. The major objective of this workshop is sensitization of the young researchers and future water professionals by the acclaimed national and international experts for understanding critical issues pertaining to resources management. This workshop will include hands on training on the popular hydrologic model- Soil and Water Assessment Tool (SWAT). The workshop is organized as a part of the ongoing multi-institutional projects funded by the SPARC initiative of the MHRD, Government of India with the objective to promote international research collaborations and engage a larger community of researchers, practitioners, NGOs, and policy makers to develop a vision for science, technologies, and policy on improving the resilience of vulnerable food, energy, and water systems in rural Indian communities.

Broad Topics

  • Hands-on training on SWAT, the hydrological modelling tool widely used to simulate hydrological processes at watershed scale and perform agricultural water resources management.
  • Introduction to food-energy-water (FEW) nexus approach in integrated watershed management and the different stakeholder driven approaches for developing resilient communities
  • Hydrological models and Decision Support Systems (DSS), and their role in achieving sustainable FEW resource development and management.
  • Case studies involving US and Indian rural watersheds and the various lessons learnt from the ongoing SPARC-MHRD projects aiming at improving the resilience of vulnerable food, energy, and water systems in rural Indian communities.
